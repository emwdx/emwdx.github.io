The 7-Day Musical Theatre Songwriting Bootcamp

Over 7 days, you’ll write a complete character establishment / “I Want” song in classic AABA-inspired form. You’ll learn how structure, character, lyric, and melody work together so that your song actually feels like it belongs in a musical.

This self-guided curriculum is inspired by professional musical theatre practice and public craft discussions from creators like Brett Boles (“The M Tea”), but is not an official course. Treat it as a framework you can adapt to your own process.

No theory degree required Lyricists & composers welcome One focused task per day
Your 7-Day Outcome
  • A clear, specific character with a strong want
  • A hook that captures their emotional core
  • An outline for an AABA structure
  • Drafted lyrics for each section
  • A singable melodic shape (basic or advanced)

You can stay at the lyric level only, or go full lyrics & music. The process works for both.

Jump to a Day

Day 1 • Why Certain Songs Feel “So Theatrical”

Today you’re putting on your creator hat: listening like a songwriter, not just a fan. You’ll start to hear the hidden framework behind great character songs.

Learning Goals
  • Understand what a character establishment / “I Want” song does in a show.
  • Identify basic AABA structure and what each section accomplishes.
  • Train your ear to notice lyric, melody, and storytelling working together.
1. Listen Like a Songwriter

Pick 2–3 songs (audio or live, not just lyrics on a page) from this list or your own favorites:

  • “Part of Your World” – The Little Mermaid
  • “The Wizard and I” – Wicked
  • “Waving Through a Window” – Dear Evan Hansen
  • “Wouldn’t It Be Loverly” – My Fair Lady
  • “Some People” – Gypsy

Don’t worry about the exact labels. Ask: What does this song tell me about the character’s world, their problem, and what they want?

2. Quick Map: What Does the Song Do?

For each song, answer in a notebook or doc:

  1. WHO is singing? One sentence.
  2. WHERE are they in the story? Very roughly (beginning, middle, end).
  3. WHAT do they want? Put it into one clear sentence starting with: “The one thing they want more than anything is…”
  4. WHY now? What pushed them to sing at this exact moment?
Notice how often the song’s title or main phrase is basically the character’s want, or the emotional lens through which they see the world.
3. A Very Light AABA Introduction

Many classic musical theatre songs (and a lot of pop standards) use AABA form:

  • A – A main idea / melody (often 8 bars)
  • A – The same idea again, with variation or new lyric
  • B – A contrasting section (bridge / “middle 8”)
  • A – A return to your main idea, often with a twist or payoff

Don’t count bars obsessively today. Just notice: When does the music feel like “home”? When does it feel like it goes somewhere new? When does it come back?

4. Creator Style Mini-Breakdown (You Do It)

Pick one song from your list and do a short, written breakdown:

  1. Underline or highlight the repeated title line / hook.
  2. Mark where you think the A / A / B / A sections start.
  3. Write 3–4 bullet points about how the song changes your understanding of the character.
5. Choose Your Show & Character

Before tomorrow, choose one character you’ll write for this week:

  • A character from an existing show you love (fan song)
  • A character from an original idea
  • A character from a book / film you want to musicalize

Write 2–3 sentences about who they are and where they are at the start of your imagined musical.

Day 1 Checklist
  • Listened to at least 2–3 great character/I Want songs
  • Answered WHO / WHERE / WHAT DO THEY WANT / WHY NOW
  • Roughly marked A / A / B / A in one song
  • Selected a character to write for this week

If you feel overwhelmed, remember: today you’re just noticing patterns. You aren’t writing yet.

Day 2 • Character & Dramatic Engine (“What Do They Want?”)

Today is all about your character’s spine. Great songs don’t just sound nice; they’re powered by a clear want, a strong obstacle, and meaningful stakes.

Learning Goals
  • Define your character’s want vs. need.
  • Clarify the obstacle and stakes.
  • Write a simple premise line that your entire song will serve.
1. Character Snapshot

Write answers for your chosen character:

  1. Who am I? (age, role, 2–3 defining traits)
  2. Where am I? (specific location & moment in the story)
  3. What do I want right now? (one clear, active desire)
  4. What’s in my way? (person, belief, rule, fear, etc.)
  5. What happens if I fail? (emotional or practical consequence)
2. Want vs. Need

Often, what the character thinks they want (their Want) is different from what they actually need to grow (their Need).

Complete these for your character:

  • WANT: “If I could just ___________, everything would finally be okay.”
  • NEED (secret / deeper truth): “I really need to learn / accept / face ___________.”
3. The One-Sentence Song Premise

Your whole song can be built on one sentence. Fill in the blanks:

Premise Sentence:
“At the beginning of the show, [Name] sings to convince themself / the world that [getting this one thing] will finally fix [this specific problem], even though what they really need is to [truth they haven’t accepted yet].”
4. Emotional Map of the Song

Rough out a 4-beat emotional journey you’ll later map onto AABA:

  1. Beat 1 (A1): “Here’s my world and what’s wrong with it.”
  2. Beat 2 (A2): “Here’s what I want and why I deserve it.”
  3. Beat 3 (B): “Here’s the scary truth / obstacle / worst-case scenario.”
  4. Beat 4 (A3): “Here’s my decision / promise / new resolve.”
5. Write in First Person

Spend 10–15 minutes free-writing as your character in first person:

  • Write “I…” sentences only.
  • Don’t rhyme. Don’t worry about line breaks.
  • Just let them rant, confess, dream, spiral.

This messy monologue is your lyric gold mine for the rest of the week.

Day 2 Checklist
  • Clear WANT and deeper NEED written out
  • Obstacle and stakes defined
  • One-sentence premise drafted
  • First-person monologue (at least 1 full page)

Tomorrow, your hook and title will grow straight out of this premise.

Day 3 • Hook, Title, and Central Idea

Great character songs have a single, memorable phrase that carries the emotional weight of the piece. Today you’ll find yours.

Learning Goals
  • Distill your character’s want into a hook line.
  • Choose a title that actually belongs in a musical theatre score.
  • Draft possible refrain lines you’ll repeat in the A sections.
1. Mine Yesterday’s Monologue

Re-read your Day 2 monologue and highlight any lines where you think: “That sounds like it could be sung.”

  • Short, emotionally loaded phrases (5–9 syllables)
  • Sentences that clearly reveal what they want
  • Lines with a strong image or metaphor
2. Draft 10 Possible Hooks

Based on what you highlighted, brainstorm at least 10 potential hook lines. Don’t judge yet.

Examples of patterns (not titles to steal, but shapes to mimic):

  • “Part of That World” → “I want to be where…”
  • “Waving Through a Window” → “I’m always on the outside looking in.”
  • “Corner of the Sky” → “There must be a place where I belong.”
3. Test Your Hook Out Loud

For each candidate hook:

  1. Say it out loud 3–4 times at different emotional intensities.
  2. Clap the natural rhythm of the words (where would the beats fall?).
  3. Ask: “Could I honestly imagine an actor belting this center stage?”
4. Choose Your Title & Refrain Line

Pick one hook to be your working title and/or repeated refrain line.

Then answer:

  • Does it clearly connect to the WANT from Day 2?
  • Is it specific to this character (not just “I want more”)?
  • Could you imagine it appearing 3–5 times in the song?
Write your chosen hook big on a page:
THIS is what my song is about.
Every A and B section choice must serve or contrast this idea.
5. Refrain Variants

Draft 3–4 variants of your hook that might appear at different moments:

  • Opening version – naive / hopeful
  • Mid-song version – frustrated / urgent
  • Bridge echo – shaken / doubtful
  • Final version – resolved / more complex
Day 3 Checklist
  • 10+ possible hook lines brainstormed
  • 1 hook chosen as working title / refrain
  • 3–4 variant versions of the hook drafted

Tomorrow, you’ll hang these hook lines on your AABA framework.

Day 4 • Building the A Sections (The World & the Want)

Today you’ll sketch the three A sections of your AABA song: A1, A2, and A3. Think of them as three passes at the same idea, each one deeper or more charged than the last.

Learning Goals
  • Understand what each A section accomplishes in a character song.
  • Outline lyric content for A1, A2, and A3.
  • Create draft lyric lines for at least one full A section.
1. Assign Jobs to A1, A2, A3

Using your emotional map from Day 2, decide:

  • A1 – Set-up: Where are we, what’s wrong, and what’s the first glimpse of the want?
  • A2 – Deepen: Why does this matter so much now? What memories, injustices, or fantasies fuel it?
  • A3 – Decision: After the bridge, what decision / promise / new internal stance do they take?
2. 4–Line Skeletons

For each A section, write a 4-line skeleton in prose (not yet fully rhymed lyrics):

  • Line 1 – Introduce the idea
  • Line 2 – Add a detail or contrast
  • Line 3 – Heighten or complicate
  • Line 4 – Land on (or near) your hook
3. Draft A1 Lyric (Rough Version)

Now, pick A1 and turn your skeleton into actual lyric lines. Don’t chase perfection—aim for:

  • Short lines (6–10 syllables is a useful range)
  • Mostly simple, conversational language
  • Your hook (exact or variant) at or near the end of the section
4. Light Touch on Rhyme

Choose one simple rhyme scheme for A1 (for example: ABAB or AABB). Try it on A2 and A3 later, even if the exact rhymes change.

Consistent patterns help the audience feel like they’re inside the character’s mind, not just hearing random lines.

5. Mark Emotional Peaks

Put a star ★ next to words or phrases that carry the most emotional charge in each A section. These will be candidates later for melodic emphasis (higher notes, longer notes, etc.).

Day 4 Checklist
  • Jobs for A1, A2, A3 clearly defined
  • 4-line prose skeleton for each A section
  • Rough lyric draft for A1 completed
  • Key emotional words / phrases starred

Tomorrow, you’ll design a contrasting B section that shakes all this up.

Day 5 • The B Section (Bridge / “Middle 8”)

In many great songs, the B section is where we step away from the familiar pattern and say: “But here’s the thing I’ve been avoiding.” It creates contrast so that the final A hits harder.

Learning Goals
  • Understand the dramatic role of the bridge.
  • Decide what truth, fear, or new angle belongs in your B section.
  • Draft B section lyrics that earn your final A.
1. What New Truth Appears Here?

Complete one of these sentences for your B section:

  • “But what if…” (introduce a fear or doubt)
  • “The thing I can’t say out loud is…”
  • “Everyone thinks I’m… but secretly I’m…”
  • “If I don’t get this, then…” (raise the stakes)
2. Contrast Checklist

Circle at least two ways your B section will contrast with the A’s:

  • Tone (hopeful → fearful, confident → uncertain)
  • Tempo / feel (driving → more suspended or vice versa)
  • Imagery (external world → internal thought, or the reverse)
  • Perspective (talking to someone vs. talking to self)
3. Draft the B Section Lyric

Write a draft B section (4–8 lines) that:

  • Tells us something we did not know from A1/A2.
  • Pushes the emotional stakes higher.
  • Points toward a choice that sets up A3.
4. Lead Back into A3

Add a final B line that naturally launches us into your last A, for example:

  • “So I’ll…”
  • “That’s why I have to…”
  • “Which means I can’t keep…”

Then jot one or two lines for how A3 will answer that line differently from A1/A2.

5. Quick A3 Touch-Up (Optional Today)

If you have time, rough in A3 using your Day 4 skeleton, now informed by the bridge. It should feel like the same song idea, but with the B section’s truth weighing on it.

Day 5 Checklist
  • Clear “new truth” chosen for B section
  • Two or more contrast choices decided (tone/tempo/imagery/etc.)
  • Draft B section lyric written
  • Line that clearly “hands off” into A3

You now have a full A–A–B–A story arc in lyric outline form.

Day 6 • Melody, Rhythm, and Prosody (Making It Sing)

Today you’ll give your lyrics a musical shape. This can be as simple or as advanced as you’re ready for. The key idea is prosody: the natural rhythm of the words matching the musical rhythm.

Learning Goals
  • Align natural speech rhythm with musical rhythm.
  • Create a simple, repeatable melodic contour for your A section.
  • Shape a contrasting melodic idea for the B section.
Option A: Lyric-First (No Theory Needed)
  1. Read your A1 lyric out loud at the intensity your character feels.
  2. Tap a steady beat on the table.
  3. Notice where stress naturally falls in each line (which syllables you hit harder).
  4. Draw little arrows above high-emotion syllables (↑ for higher note, → for held note, ↓ for lower).

You’ve just created a contour map for your melody. Even if someone else later sets it fully to music, you’ve indicated where the line should rise, fall, and sit.

Option B: Simple Chord Loop (For Basic Players)

If you play piano / guitar, choose a simple 4-chord loop as your A section “home,” for example:

  • In C: C – Am – Dm – G
  • In G: G – Em – Am – D

Sing your A1 lyric over this loop. Don’t worry about “writing” yet—just play the loop and let your voice find a pattern that feels natural and repeatable.

B Section Contrast (Musically)

To make your B feel different, you can:

  • Start the melody higher or lower than the A.
  • Use longer notes where A is busy, or vice versa.
  • Change the chord pattern slightly (for example, start on chord ii or IV instead of I).
Mark Key Melodic Moments

Choose at least three moments where the melody should do something special:

  • The first time the hook appears
  • The most painful or vulnerable line in the bridge
  • The final version of the hook in A3

For each, note whether you want a higher pitch, a longer note, or a change in rhythm to make it land.

Day 6 Checklist
  • Speech rhythm mapped for A1 (and ideally A2/A3)
  • Simple melodic contour or chord loop chosen for A section
  • Clear musical contrast idea for B section
  • 3 key “melodic spotlight” moments identified

Record a quick voice memo of your A and B ideas so you don’t lose them.

Day 7 • Refinement, Clarity, and Sharing

Today you’ll clean up your lyrics, tighten your structure, and get the song ready to share— even if it’s still a draft. Musical theatre is a collaborative art; the goal is to get your song clear enough that others can see the show you’re imagining.

Learning Goals
  • Ensure the song tells a clear, specific story.
  • Polish lyrics for singability and clarity.
  • Prepare a simple way to share (sung or read) with others.
1. Read/Sing the Whole Song Through

Perform your song (spoken or sung) from top to bottom:

  1. A1 – A2 – B – A3 in order
  2. Use your hook where you planned to
  3. Mark any moment where you stumble or feel confused
2. Story Clarity Pass

Ask yourself (or a friend) these questions:

  • “Do I understand who this person is?”
  • “Can I answer what they want in one sentence?”
  • “Do I feel the stakes / why this matters?”
  • “Does something shift between the beginning and the end?”
3. Lyric Cleanup

Look for:

  • Clutter: Can you cut extra adjectives/adverbs?
  • Vagueness: Swap “things / stuff / it” for specific nouns.
  • Unsingable consonant clusters: Adjust to be easy to sing.
  • Inconsistent imagery: Keep metaphors coherent within the song.
4. Final Hook Check

Make sure your hook:

  • Appears at least 3 times across the song (or strong variants do).
  • Feels slightly different emotionally in the final A than in the first.
  • Lines up with what the audience now believes the character wants/needs.
5. Shareable Version

Prepare a simple way to share your work:

  • Lyrics only: clean document with sections labeled A1 / A2 / B / A3.
  • Lyrics + melody: lead sheet, rough notation, chorded lyric sheet, or voice memo.
  • Optional: a short note at the top: “This is [Character]’s ‘I Want’ song from [Show].”
6. Reflection

Answer for yourself:

  • What part of this process unlocked the most for you?
  • Which step felt hardest—and what would you like help with next?
  • What’s one thing you’ll try differently on your next character song?

You’ve now written a full character establishment song in a week. Future drafts will get faster and more confident as your ear for structure keeps growing.

Day 7 Checklist
  • Full AABA lyric read/sung through at least once
  • Clarity & lyric clean-up pass completed
  • Hook placements and emotional evolution checked
  • Shareable version prepared (lyrics and/or demo)

Keep this song as Draft 1. Professional writers often revise songs dozens of times—even when the framework is solid.
